A beginner's guide to the entities, acronyms, and terms behind building AI products responsibly. This glossary covers the vocabulary you'll run into most often when learning AI governance — who the regulators are, what the major frameworks require, and what the recurring terms actually mean.

## Governing Bodies & Entities

**European Commission** — The EU's executive body. Drafted and enforces the EU AI Act, and issues the implementing guidance that shapes how the Act applies in practice.

**EU AI Office** — The body inside the European Commission responsible for supervising and enforcing rules for general-purpose AI models under the EU AI Act, and for coordinating enforcement across EU member states.

**National Competent Authority** — The government body each EU member state designates to enforce the AI Act within its borders — handling complaints, investigations, and penalties for AI systems operating in that country.

**Notified Body** — An independent organization authorized to assess whether a high-risk AI system meets the EU AI Act's conformity requirements before it goes to market. Required for certain high-risk categories.

**NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology)** — A U.S. federal agency that develops voluntary technical standards, including the AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) widely used by organizations building AI responsibly.

**ISO / IEC** — The International Organization for Standardization and the International Electrotechnical Commission jointly publish ISO/IEC 42001, the international certification standard for AI management systems.

**Market Surveillance Authority** — The enforcement arm that monitors AI systems already in use, investigates complaints, and can order corrective action or withdrawal from the market.

## Frameworks & Standards

**EU AI Act** — The European Union's comprehensive law regulating AI, in force since 2024. It sorts AI systems into risk tiers (unacceptable, high, limited, minimal) and attaches different obligations to each.

**Digital Omnibus (on AI)** — A 2026 legislative package amending the EU AI Act to defer several high-risk compliance deadlines and simplify parts of the regulation. The European Parliament and Council gave final approval in June 2026 and the amending act was signed 8 July 2026; as of this writing it awaits publication in the Official Journal, the last step before entering into force. Treat any specific EU AI Act compliance date as provisional until that publication lands.

**NIST AI RMF (AI Risk Management Framework)** — A voluntary U.S. framework organized around four functions — Govern, Map, Measure, Manage — that helps organizations identify and reduce AI-related risks throughout a system's lifecycle.

**ISO/IEC 42001** — The first international standard for an AI Management System (AIMS). Organizations can be certified against it, similar to how ISO 9001 certifies quality management systems.

**GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)** — The EU's foundational data privacy law. It predates the AI Act but applies whenever an AI system processes personal data, making the two frameworks tightly linked.

**OECD AI Principles** — A set of intergovernmental principles for trustworthy AI, first adopted in 2019, that influenced the drafting of both the EU AI Act and NIST's framework.

## Common Acronyms

**AIMS (AI Management System)** — The organizational structure — policies, roles, processes — a company puts in place to govern how it builds and deploys AI. ISO/IEC 42001 certifies AIMS maturity.

**HITL (Human-in-the-Loop)** — A design requirement that a human reviews or can override an AI system's output before it takes effect. Mandatory for many high-risk use cases under the EU AI Act.

**PII (Personally Identifiable Information)** — Any data that can identify a specific person, directly or indirectly. Central to GDPR compliance and to the EU AI Act's data governance requirements.

**GPAI (General-Purpose AI)** — An AI model trained to perform a wide range of tasks rather than one narrow function (e.g., large language models). The EU AI Act creates a distinct set of obligations specifically for GPAI providers.

**DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment)** — A GDPR-required assessment of privacy risk before deploying a system likely to affect individuals' data rights.

**FRIA (Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment)** — An EU AI Act requirement for certain deployers of high-risk AI systems to assess the system's impact on people's fundamental rights before deployment.

**RMF (Risk Management Framework)** — Shorthand generally referring to NIST's AI RMF, though the term applies to any structured process for identifying and mitigating risk.

**CE Marking** — The certification mark showing a product (including certain high-risk AI systems) meets EU health, safety, and environmental requirements, including the AI Act.

## Common Terms & Phrases

**Risk Tier** — The EU AI Act's core classification concept. Every AI system falls into one of four tiers — unacceptable (banned), high (heavily regulated), limited (transparency-only), or minimal (unregulated) — with obligations scaled to the tier.

**Provider** — Under the EU AI Act, the organization that develops an AI system or has it developed, and places it on the market. Carries the heaviest compliance obligations.

**Deployer** — The organization that uses an AI system in the course of its own operations, as opposed to building it. Deployers have lighter but still meaningful obligations, especially for high-risk systems.

**Conformity Assessment** — The formal process of verifying that a high-risk AI system meets all applicable legal requirements before it can be placed on the market or put into service.

**Transparency Obligation** — A requirement that people be told they're interacting with an AI system, or that AI-generated content be labeled as such. Applies broadly, even to non-high-risk systems.

**Model Card** — A short, standardized document describing an AI model's intended use, training data, performance, and limitations — written for transparency, not for marketing.

**System Card** — Similar to a model card but describing an entire AI system, including how a model is deployed, monitored, and governed in production.

**Serious Incident** — Under EU AI Act Article 73, an event where an AI system causes death, serious harm to health, serious disruption to critical infrastructure, or a serious violation of fundamental rights — triggering mandatory reporting.

**Post-Market Monitoring** — The ongoing obligation to track a deployed AI system's real-world performance and report problems, rather than treating compliance as a one-time, pre-launch checkbox.

**Prohibited Practice** — An AI use case banned outright under the EU AI Act's "unacceptable risk" tier — for example, social scoring by governments or manipulative subliminal techniques.

**Substantial Modification** — A change to an AI system significant enough that it must be re-assessed for compliance, as if it were a new system.

**Bias** — A systematic skew in an AI system's outputs that unfairly favors or disadvantages a group, typically traced back to training data, model design, or how the system is used.

**Explainability** — The degree to which a person can understand why an AI system produced a specific output — a prerequisite for meaningful human oversight.

**Interpretability** — A closely related but more technical concept: how well the internal mechanics of a model can be understood, as opposed to just its outputs.

**Data Minimization** — The principle, rooted in GDPR and echoed in the EU AI Act, that a system should collect and process only the personal data strictly necessary for its purpose.

**Human Oversight** — A requirement that AI systems, particularly high-risk ones, are designed so people can effectively monitor, intervene in, or stop them.

**Accountability** — The principle that a specific person or organization — not "the algorithm" — is responsible for an AI system's outcomes and compliance.

**Governance** — The overall structure of policies, roles, and processes an organization uses to ensure its AI systems are built and operated responsibly. The foundation every other term in this glossary supports.

**Guardrails** — Technical or procedural controls built into an AI system to keep its behavior within defined, safe boundaries.

**Red Teaming** — Deliberately probing an AI system for weaknesses, harmful outputs, or exploitable behavior before or after deployment, to find problems ahead of real-world use.

**Shadow AI** — AI tools or systems in use within an organization without formal review, approval, or inclusion in its governance inventory — a common source of ungoverned risk.

**Algorithmic Impact Assessment** — A structured evaluation of how an AI system might affect the people subject to its decisions, conducted before deployment.

**Trustworthy AI** — A shorthand goal shared across the OECD Principles, NIST AI RMF, and EU AI Act: AI that is lawful, ethical, and technically robust.

**AI Inventory** — An organization's authoritative registry of every AI system, model, and integration in active use — the reference point governance frameworks assume exists before anything else can be assessed.
