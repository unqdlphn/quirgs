# Pitch Writing Guide

Structure, tone, and templates for sync licensing pitches.

---

## Pitch Principles

1. **One track per email.** Send one pitch email per track. Sending five tracks in one email forces the supervisor to do the curation work you should be doing — it signals you haven't thought carefully about the fit.

2. **Reference the brief directly.** Every pitch should demonstrate you read the brief. Use the brief's own language. If the brief says "bittersweet nostalgia," your pitch should say "bittersweet nostalgia" — not "sad but hopeful."

3. **Lead with the fit, not the credentials.** What matters is whether the track works for the placement. Your background matters second.

4. **Keep it short.** A music supervisor handles hundreds of pitches. Three paragraphs maximum. If it takes longer to describe why the track fits, it probably doesn't fit well enough.

5. **Make delivery frictionless.** Include a streaming link (no download required to preview), clear duration, and BPM. If stems or multitracks are available, say so — it's a positive signal.

6. **No generic superlatives.** "Emotional," "cinematic," "epic," and "perfect for your project" without specifics are credibility destroyers. Replace with specific observations.

## Pitch Structure

### Subject Line
Format: `[Track Title] — [One-line fit rationale]`

Examples:
- `"Hollow Roads" — Instrumental, 92 BPM, melancholic acoustic for the Act 2 driving scene`
- `"Fracture" — Uptempo electronic, builds to climax at 1:45, matches the brief's escalation arc`

### Opening (1 sentence)
Acknowledge the project and establish a brief connection. Do not begin with "I hope this email finds you well."

Good: `Reaching out re: the Apex campaign brief — I have a track that fits the "quiet confidence" tone you described.`

Avoid: `My name is [Name] and I am a music producer/composer/publisher based in [City].` (That's in your signature — don't repeat it.)

### Track Paragraph (3–5 sentences)
- Title, artist, duration, BPM, key (if relevant to mood)
- Why it specifically fits this brief: mood, tempo, instrumentation, lyrical theme
- One concrete observation about how the track functions in context (e.g., "The track opens sparsely and builds to a full arrangement at 2:10 — timing that lines up naturally with a scene climax")
- No adjectives without backup: say "92 BPM minor-key acoustic" instead of "emotional and intimate"

### Availability Paragraph (2–3 sentences)
- Confirm the track is available for the proposed use
- State territory (worldwide or specific)
- State exclusivity stance (available non-exclusive, or open to exclusive negotiation)
- Note any material clearance flags (no samples, fully owned, etc.)

### Delivery Line (1–2 sentences)
- Streaming link (preferred: Soundcloud with download disabled for preview, or Spotify)
- Note whether stems, multitracks, or alternate edits are available upon request

### Closing (1 sentence)
Clean and professional. Include your name and direct contact.

## Email Template

```
Subject: "[Track Title]" — [One-line rationale]

Hi [Name / "there"],

[Opening — 1 sentence, reference the project or brief]

"[Track Title]" by [Artist] is a [duration], [BPM] BPM [genre/mood descriptor].
[Why it fits — 2–3 specific sentences referencing the brief's language].
[Concrete structural observation — timing, arrangement, moment that serves the scene].

The track is fully owned and available [non-exclusively / for exclusive licensing negotiation]
for [territory] use. [Any relevant licensing note — no samples, stems available, etc.]

Listen: [Link]
[Stems / alternate edits available on request — if applicable]

[Your name]
[Title]
[Contact / website]
```

## Pitch Tone by Media Type

Adjust register slightly based on the placement type:

| Media type | Tone | What to emphasize |
|---|---|---|
| Advertising / commercial | Confident, business-forward | Brand alignment, tempo precision, no lyric conflicts |
| Film / drama series | Cinematic, scene-aware | Emotional arc, how track evolves, silence/space in arrangement |
| Documentary | Direct, factual | Authenticity, instrumentation, avoiding overly "produced" sound |
| Trailer | Punchy, impact-focused | Build, climax moment, dramatic dynamics, tension |
| Video game | Technical, atmosphere-aware | Loop points, seamless endings, variation potential |
| Social media / branded content | Casual, current | Trend alignment, energy, short-form edit availability |

## Red Flags to Check Before Sending

Before sending any pitch:

- [ ] No misspellings of the supervisor's name or project title
- [ ] Streaming link is live and playable (test it before sending)
- [ ] Track is actually available — confirmed with rights holders if co-written or co-owned
- [ ] No unresolved sample clearances
- [ ] Pitch is specific to this brief — not a copy-paste from a previous pitch
- [ ] Subject line is unique and descriptive (not "Music Submission" or "New Track")
- [ ] No attachment — links only unless the supervisor specifically requests a file

---

## Following Up

If no response after 10–14 business days:

- One follow-up email is acceptable. Keep it to two sentences.
- Do not re-pitch the same track unless the brief is still active and the first email bounced or was demonstrably not delivered.
- If the supervisor passes, do not argue. Thank them and ask if there's anything else you can send for future briefs.

Do not follow up more than once. Music supervisors remember persistent submitters — negatively.
